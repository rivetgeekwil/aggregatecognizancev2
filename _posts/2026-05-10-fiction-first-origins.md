In a chat that I'm in, several people, inspired by my last blog post and discussions around fiction first, did some digital archaeology. I was honestly going to do this myself and write a post about it, but they decided to perform a group action and it greatly accelerated the process of finding the information. It didn't take as long as I thought it would, and the results aren't quite what I was expecting. I asked if it was okay if I compiled all of this into a coherent timeline of sorts, and here we are.

What does "fiction first" mean, and where did the phrase come from? It's one of the most commonly used terms in tabletop RPG design discourse today, but its origins turn out to be surprisingly murky. What follows is a timeline reconstructed from a collective deep-dive into old forum archives, game texts, blog posts, and personal correspondence, in an attempt to trace a piece of jargon back to its roots.

Before we dig in, it's worth noting that "fiction first" carries at least two distinct meanings in modern usage, and untangling them is part of the story.

The first is **procedural**: you begin in the fiction, turn to the mechanics only when needed, and return to the fiction to interpret the result. This is the sense used in *Blades in the Dark* and, arguably, the original technical meaning. As Chris Clouser put it in his 2013 *Sixth World* hack: "Everything that happens in a session starts with the fiction, proceeds to rules (if necessary), and ends with the fiction."

The second is **hierarchical**: when the fiction and the rules conflict, the fiction wins. This is the sense popularized more recently, particularly in actual-play and trindie communities — what Brand Robins calls "the Sorenson Scenario," after podcaster Sam Sorenson's use of the term.

Tthe procedural meaning likely came first, with the hierarchical meaning accruing later as the term spread into broader use. The evidence uncovered broadly supports this, though the picture is far from complete.

# Two Terms, Two Timelines

One of the key findings of this research is that the history of "fiction first" is really the history of two related but distinct lexical shifts, and you can't understand the second without the first. First, "the fiction" had to become jargon — a specific term of art replacing the Forge's older "Shared Imagined Space." Only after that shift was complete could "fiction first" emerge as a compound concept.[^1]

The timeline below traces both shifts, since they are deeply entangled.

## Pre-2006: The Conceptual Groundwork

The underlying ideas long predate either phrase. The Forge's theoretical vocabulary included concepts like "Shared Imagined Space" (SIS) and the positioning of fortune (randomizers) relative to narration — "fortune at the beginning, middle, or end." Two of those three fortune positions were arguably "fiction first" already, since you started in the narration and shifted to the randomizer only later.

An early Forge thread on [*Legends of Alyria* and Traits](http://www.indie-rpgs.com/archive/index.php?topic=27171.15;wap2) (2008) explored the question of whether traits constrain the fiction or the fiction constrains traits — what Seth Ben-Ezra framed as "Before" versus "After" Traits. Brand highlighted Seth's formulation as an early attempt at the same distinction: "'Before' Traits" meant that the fiction determines whether a trait is applicable, while "'After' Traits" meant traits shape the fiction after the fact. As Brand noted, "take away some of the Forge language and paradigm, and that whole thread is very similar to a lot of conversations about the interactions between fiction and mechanics right now."[^2]

## 2006–2008: "The Fiction" Enters the Lexicon

Before roughly 2006–2007, most games used "fiction" in the ordinary literary sense. Games from that era or earlier — *My Life With Master*, *Dust Devils*, *Trollbabe*, *The Mountain Witch*, *Primetime Adventures* — use "fiction" only when talking about the type of fiction that inspired the game, not in the sense of the shared game world. Several other Forge keystones, including *Polaris*, *Dogs in the Vineyard*, *Bliss Stage*, and *Grey Ranks*, don't use the word at all.

The shift to using "the fiction" as jargon — meaning the game world and its events as they unfold at the table, absorbing some of the meaning of "Shared Imagined Space" — appears to have happened through blog discussions and forum conversations on the Forge, Storygames, Knife Fight, and surrounding blogs between 2006 and 2008, driven by voices like Emily Care Boss, Meg Baker, and others.

Early published examples include *Steal Away Jordan* (2007), which uses "fiction" firmly in the SIS sense, and *In a Wicked Age* (2007), which contains phrases like "you make the world of your game's fiction more intimate" — though even here the usage isn't fully "jargon-ified" yet. A few more casual but potentially influenced uses appear around 2009, such as *A Penny for My Thoughts*. Ron Edwards uses the term somewhat in the annotations for the 2012 edition of *Sorcerer*, but not in the original text.

This shift can be seen strikingly between editions of *Burning Wheel*. In the first edition, the word "fiction" appears in phrases like "players take on the roles of characters inspired by history and works of fantasy fiction". But in the later *Burning Wheel Codex*, the same word has transformed: "This process also produces a result in the shared fiction of the game: something happens in the story," and "Lifestyle maintenance cycles depend on the pace and passage of time in the fiction." The *Codex* even includes a footnote about "fictional positioning" in Adam Kobel's foreword. This is the same game from the same designer, with an entirely different use of the word.

Emily Care Boss's 2006 article in *Push* magazine doesn't use the phrase "fiction first," but lays the groundwork for discussions about fictional positioning — the idea that a character's available actions are constrained by their situation within the shared fiction. Emily Care Boss was leading conversations around fictional positioning from roughly 2006 to 2010, with John Harper and others participating. Emily's 2007 post [Leverage in gaming](http://fairgame-rpgs.com/index.php/fairgame/thread/114) on the Fairgame Archive explores what she calls "story capital" — the idea that fictional positioning creates real strategic weight in play. As she put it: "Kickers, interests, conflicts, advantages, attributes: all these mechanical things are there to help us find story capital. And also, mechanics enforce the weight of elements we make up to act as leverage." This concept — that fictional elements carry real mechanical and strategic weight — became a key building block in the conversations that would eventually produce "fiction first."

Brand Robins' own 2008 blog post on Yudhishthira's Dice, titled [An RPG is a game where the fiction is part of the rules](https://web.archive.org/web/20081210140559/http://games.spaceanddeath.com/yudhishthirasdice/133), captures the zeitgeist of those discussions, and Brand notes he wasn't the leading edge of the conversation around "fictional position" or "the fiction".

## 2008: Vincent Baker's "Respecting the Fiction" and the Earliest Known Use of the Phrase

A crucial 2008 post on Vincent Baker's *Anyway* blog, [Respecting the fiction](http://lumpley.com/index.php/anyway/thread/350) laid out many of the ideas the phrase would later crystallize. Vincent described how indie RPGs had become "hypermechanized" — like a couple dancing strict ballroom, where the game mechanics are the man and he leads. He argued this wasn't a problem per se, but that promoting this feature up to a universal design *principle* was a mistake. In his own games, he wrote, "the fiction is relatively coequal with the mechanics," and in *Poison'd*, "the fiction actually leads."

John Harper responded in [the comments](http://lumpley.com/index.php/anyway/thread/350#10327) with a phrase that would prove prophetic: "'Leading with the fiction,' is a great way to say it, which I will happily steal." Harper went on to observe that some games want you to lead with the fiction and sometimes with mechanics, and that figuring out which is which "comprises a huge chunk of 'how we play' and which games click for you and yours." This 2008 exchange appears to be the moment when Harper first latched onto the idea he would later codify in *Blades in the Dark*. If you're specifically interested in the definition that ends up in Blades — the procedural one — this Vincent/Harper exchange is where you find its conceptual framework, even if the vocabulary isn't there yet.

Meanwhile, on the Forge, Jesse A. (who was involved in these very discussions researching ""fiction first" posted a thread titled [System Transforms Situation... And Situation Informs System?](http://www.indie-rpgs.com/forge/index.php?topic=27234.msg257823#msg257823), and wrote:

> I've seen two basic attitudes about that. One I call System First, the other I call Fiction First. The System First approach looks at this and says I want my character to be Mean a lot so I will make his highest score Mean... The Fiction First approach looks at this and says when my character is Mean, I want him to be scary effective, but the score itself says NOTHING about how often or how much they desire the character to be Mean.

Vincent Baker's own phrase during this period was "lead with the fiction," which was used frequently on Storygames.com from 2009 onward. Vincent had repeatedly used the phrase "fictional causes" during a 2008 interview on Clyde Rhoer's podcast. While these aren't identical to "fiction first," they clearly influenced the thinking.

The meaning here isn't quite the later procedural or hierarchical definition — it's more about player attitude toward the relationship between scores and character behavior — but the specific language is there. In the same thread, Jesse explores the broader idea of how "the emotional commitment of the players to the SIS can be relied upon as a limiter for engaging the more mechanical aspects of the game."

These early Forge uses of "fiction first" carry a different meaning than the term would take on once it entered wider publication years later. The early uses were embedded in long-running theoretical conversations where participants shared a degree of joint reference — making the terms less crisp than their later, published formations, but also more tightly connected to specific debates about how play actually works.

## 2009: The Phrase Surfaces Again

Callan S. used "fiction first" in posts on the Forge archive in 2009, in threads about [the nature of "system"](http://indie-rpgs.com/archive/index.php?topic=28880.msg271023#msg271023) and discussions of [*Spione*](http://indie-rpgs.com/archive/index.php?topic=28608.msg269477#msg269477). Callan's usage is more about where rules are introduced and called upon in reference to events in the fiction. It's closer to the procedural meaning, but still entangled with the same logic previously applied to the fortune-positioning conversations.

## 2010–2011: Apocalypse World, "Color First," and the Paradigm Shift

*Apocalypse World* (2010) proved to be a watershed, not for using the phrase "fiction first" (which it doesn't contain), but for crystallizing many of the ideas the phrase would come to describe. The game talks extensively about "the fiction" and commits to the logic of the fictional setting and characters, but some of its text actually runs against the grain of what "fiction first" later came to mean. Clocks tick and trigger events without fictional input; checking a box gives you a gang. Things work both ways, which is why Vincent Baker has [disavowed the term](https://bsky.app/profile/lumpley.bsky.social/post/3llkfqyha7k2p) in connection with Apocalypse World. As Vincent put it: "PbtA doesn't begin and end with the fiction, it begins and ends with the conversation. 'Fiction first' is an idea that came to PbtA later, from I don't really know where."

By 2010, "the fiction" in its modern jargon sense had spread into many Forge-diaspora games, driven largely by the paradigm shift that *Apocalypse World* caused and carried through the Storygames and Knife Fight forums. But "fiction first" as a discrete term was still finding its footing. During this period, the surrounding discourse included substantial discussion of "Color First Design" and play, a parallel concept that was part of the same constellation of ideas about what should lead or drive the experience at the table.  A 2010 essay collection called *The Bones*, featuring writers from more traditional and trindie backgrounds like Kenneth Hite and Will Hindmarch, discusses similar concepts without using the term at all.

John Harper's 2011 blog [post](https://mightyatom.blogspot.com/2011/05/apocalypse-world-guide-to-hard-moves.html) about hard moves in Apocalypse Worlddoesn't use "fiction first" either, but it contains the core idea: "when you engage with the game start with the fiction, roll some dice, and look back and center what happens in the fiction."

Also in 2011, a Simon C. [commented](http://lumpley.com/index.php/anyway/thread/588#15307) on Vincent Baker's *Anyway* blog, in a thread about Ben Lehman's "Rules and their Functions" guest post. Simon wrote about classifying rules along several axes, including what he described as "Fiction first–Real life first" — whether a rule asks questions of the fiction or of real-life things (dice, numbers on sheets, who's sitting where). As Simon put it: "There's this whole other bit we haven't really talked about, which is rules which ask questions of the fiction, vs. rules which ask questions of real-life things." Simon again defined "fiction first" as in a [discussion]((https://lumpley.games/thebarf/index.php?topic=344.5%3Bwap2) around "Color First Design.". These uses represent the earliest known instances of the phrase being deployed with real taxonomic precision. Simon's usage on *Anyway* makes the distinction between fiction and reality rather than fiction and mechanics, which is a position that isn't strongl carried forward.

Vincent Baker draws a similar distinction in his [Designing PbtA series](https://lumpley.games/2019/12/30/powered-by-the-apocalypse-part-1/), preferring to talk about "real" things rather than "mechanical" ones because it's sharper, and focuses on what the player is actually interacting with. Apocalyope Worlds playtesters may have used "fiction first" as another way to characterize Apocalype World's "to do it, do it" principle. 

### 2012–2013: The Term Coalesces

*Dungeon World* (2012) expanded the conversation enormously, pushing Forge-diaspora ideas into contact with more traditional RPG communities. The game talks extensively about fiction and its relationship to mechanics. *Dungeon World* was likely responsible for "the fiction" hitting games outside the storygames scene. Its community was a major vector for the development of "fiction first" as a concept, with the Something Awful forums playing a notable role alongside the Storygames and Apocalypse World forums.

Sage LaTorra's 2012 blog post "[A 16 HP Dragon](https://www.latorra.org/2012/05/15/a-16-hp-dragon/)," quoting a forum post by Stras Acimovic, became a touchstone for the community. The post made the case that a dragon could be terrifying with only 16 hit points if you let the fiction do the heavy lifting — describing the dragon's breath shaping fire through the streets, ripping through mail armor, and people burning alive while holding children turning to ash in their arms. As stras wrote: "The moral of the story is it's not about the hitpoints." Brand recalled it dominating community discussion for about a year.

The "How to Play Dungeon World" zine (2013) comes very close to stating the principle without using the exact phrase: "When in doubt, look to your established game fiction first to see what makes sense."

Chris Clouser's *Sixth World* Shadowrun hack for Dungeon World (2013) also contains a clear, well-defined use of the term: "Everything that happens in a session of Sixth World starts with the fiction, proceeds to rules (if necessary), and ends with the fiction."

By 2013, people on the Storygames forum were already pushing back, asking about "[fiction last — what does it ruin for you?](https://web.archive.org/web/20191221130201/http://www.story-games.com/forums/discussion/18419/fiction-last-what-does-it-ruin-for-you)" — which, as Brand pointed out, presupposes a "fiction first" to react against. In [page 3 of that same thread](https://web.archive.org/web/20191222185554/http://www.story-games.com/forums/discussion/18419/fiction-last-what-does-it-ruin-for-you/p3), multiple posters attributed the concept to Vincent Baker specifically, lending support to Clyde's early suggestion that Vincent was the originator (or at least the figure most associated with the underlying ideas).

Brand noted that from 2012 to 2014, "fiction first" appeared across these forums but less frequently than he would have expected — it was in the mix, but far from the dominant terminology. During this same period, however, John Harper was becoming a leading voice in the broader conversation. Having been involved since the early days of the "leading with the fiction" discussion, Harper was putting significant effort between 2011 and 2014 into both developing his own games and teaching others to play AW and PbtA games, developing frameworks to discuss the paradigm differences between those modes of play and more traditional approaches. Brand found few examples of Harper using the specific term "fiction first" in this period — possibly because much of that discourse happened on Google+ and other now-shuttered platforms — but Harper was clearly developing the thinking that would culminate in Blades.

### 2015–2016: Blades in the Dark Makes It Official

Brand surveyed first-generation PbtA games (*Apocalypse World*, *Dungeon World*, *Monsterhearts*, *Sagas of the Icelanders*) and found that while they all discuss fiction and its relationship to mechanics, none use "Fiction First" as a term of art. Even into 2015–2016, it remains rare in PbtA texts.

The earliest published, clearly defined use of "Fiction First" as a specific design term that Brand could identify is ***Blades in the Dark*** by John Harper. While blog posts and forum discussions used the phrase earlier, Blades appears to be the first game to print a definition of "Fiction-First gaming" as a named concept.

As Brand concluded in his final analysis, while Blades doesn't seem to be the origin of the term, it played a critical role in giving it a solid lexical definition that could be shared across multiple communities. Before Blades, different people and communities used "fiction first" in slightly different senses — some to discuss design principles, others to describe methodologies of play. Blades gave it enough form and specificity that it could travel.

Harper's enthusiasm for the idea is visible all the way back to his [2008 comment](http://lumpley.com/index.php/anyway/thread/350#10327) about "leading with the fiction." Even if Blades isn't the absolute point of origin, Brand concluded, "it is clearly a primary vector" — the game that carried the term into wide circulation within the Forged in the Dark family and beyond.

### 2019–2020: Mainstream Adoption

By 2019, games outside the Forge diaspora were using "the fiction" in the modern sense — Brand cited *Over the Edge 3rd Edition* as an example, and from there noted it spreading into Monte Cook's work and other trindie spaces. *Fate Condensed* (2020) uses "fiction first" as normalized terminology — a notable contrast with the original *Fate Core* (2013), which predated the completion of the lexical shift. The [Fate SRD](https://fate-srd.com/odds-ends/fiction-first-fiction-rules-interaction-and-nonsensical-results) now includes a page on the concept, defining it as the idea that character actions should start with the fiction, be described in terms of the fiction, and only then be interpreted into mechanics. Jesse A. confirmed that this text was added later and does not appear in the original 2013 *Fate Core* book, despite initial assumptions to the contrary. Brand verified this by checking the original Core files: "I'm in the original Core files from 2013 and it's not there." (Jesse noted ruefully that AI-powered Google search had led him astray on this point.)

At this point, Brand considered both "the fiction" and "fiction first" to be pretty mainstream in RPG talk outside of pure D&D and very traditional circles.

## What We Didn't Find

Several participants emphasized the limits of this research. Google+, the Knife Fight forums, Something Awful's RPG threads, and other private or defunct discussion spaces were major venues for RPG design discourse during the critical 2008–2013 period, and their contents are largely lost. Brand expressed frustration at the "death of the internet" — conversations that shaped the term's development simply no longer exist in searchable form. The collapse of Google+ and the migration of conversation to decentralized platforms made the post-2014 trail particularly difficult to follow.

The Storygames forum archive survives and is searchable via a [GitHub repository](https://github.com/jeffschecter/storygames) maintained by Jeff Schecter, but hasn't been exhaustively searched for this project. The Forge archive is searchable but clunky.

## Summary of Findings

The phrase "fiction first" appears to have emerged gradually between 2008 and 2013, but it could only do so after a prior lexical shift — the replacement of "Shared Imagined Space" with "the fiction" — had laid the groundwork. That first shift happened between roughly 2006 and 2010, driven by conversations on the Forge, Storygames, Knife Fight, and surrounding blogs, and carried into publication through games like *Steal Away Jordan*, *In a Wicked Age*, and eventually *Apocalypse World*.

The earliest known use of the exact phrase "fiction first" is Jesse A.'s [2008 Forge post](http://www.indie-rpgs.com/forge/index.php?topic=27234.msg257823#msg257823), followed by Callan S. in [2009](http://indie-rpgs.com/archive/index.php?topic=28608.msg269477#msg269477) and Simon C. in [2011](http://lumpley.com/index.php/anyway/thread/588#15307) (on *Anyway*) and [2011](https://lumpley.games/thebarf/index.php?topic=344.5%3Bwap2) (on the AW forums), though none of these uses carry quite the same meaning the term holds today. The concepts behind it were being actively developed by Emily Care Boss, Vincent Baker, John Harper, Meg Baker, Eero Tuovinen, and others throughout this period, with Vincent's "[Respecting the fiction](http://lumpley.com/index.php/anyway/thread/350)" post and his phrase "lead with the fiction" serving as likely direct ancestors.

The first published game to define "Fiction First" as a named design principle appears to be *Blades in the Dark* by John Harper, making it the primary vehicle through which the term entered widespread use. Blades didn't originate the term, but it gave the concept a stable enough definition to travel across communities that had previously been using it in slightly different ways.

Whether the term has a single originator remains unclear. As Brand put it: "There is both a timing and a usage gap" between the earliest forum appearances and the later codified meaning. The phrase likely coalesced from overlapping conversations rather than springing from any single source. And as Brand observed in closing, the decentralized nature of modern online discourse means that we've come full circle: "It's now a pretty common term, but... once again we're often defining it in different formations."
